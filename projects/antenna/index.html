EArly predictioN of immunoTherapy inducEd toxicity on [18F]FDG-PET scaNs in caNcer pAtients (ANTENNA)

September 2025 - February 2026

This work was done in collaboration with Simion Polivencu, Cristian Mihaiescu and Kim Wattez
Under the supervision of Erik Aarntzen1, Juan-Diego Cardenas-Cartagena2 and Marleen Schippers2
With the support of Lucie Hijmering-Kappelle1 and Frederike Bensch1
1University Medical Center Groningen
2Department of Artificial Intelligence - Faculty of Science and Engineering - University of Groningen

The scope of this project was the development of a machine learning system to predict the severity of immunotherapy-related adverse events (toxicity) in patients with metastatic lung cancer using clinical data and PET/CT scans. The research question investigates whether metabolic patterns observed in baseline [18F]FDG-PET scans of organs can predict the severity of immune-related side effects following treatment.

The motivation for the project stems from the growing use of immunotherapy as a treatment for late stage lung cancer. While immunotherapy can significantly improve patient survival compared to traditional chemotherapy, it can also trigger immune-related adverse events (toxicity), which range from mild to life-threatening. Currently, clinicians cannot reliably predict these side effects based on visual inspection of scans or patient records alone. Therefore, an automated predictive system could support clinical decision-making and improve patient outcomes.

The problem is a binary classification task, where the model predicts whether a patient will experience mild or severe toxicity. The dataset consists of two main components: clinical data (including medical history and blood tests) and PET/CT scan-derived features. Although the clinical dataset originally contained over 500 features, only 16 were selected based on domain knowledge and prior research to reduce dimensionality and improve model performance, given the very small dataset. From the imaging data, features such as organ volume and metabolic activity (measured via Standardized Uptake Value, or SUV) were extracted.

A major challenge in the project is the limited and imbalanced dataset. The final sample included a very small number of patients, with a strong class imbalance between mild and severe cases. This constraint influenced both model selection and evaluation strategy. The priority metrics used were recall (especially for severe cases) and ROC-AUC, as these better reflect performance in imbalanced and clinically sensitive contexts.

Several machine learning models were implemented and compared, including Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and XGBoost.

To ensure reliability for clinical use, the project incorporated uncertainty quantification techniques. Metrics such as Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and Brier Score were used to assess how well model confidence aligned with actual outcomes. Calibration methods and visual analyses were applied to improve and interpret model predictions. Feature importance analysis further revealed consistent biological signals, identifying liver enzymes (ALAT, ASAT) and spleen metabolic activity as key predictors of toxicity.

The data preprocessing pipeline involved handling both imaging and tabular data. PET/CT scans required reconstruction from DICOM files, segmentation, and extraction of quantitative features. Clinical data required extensive cleaning due to missing values and inconsistencies. Care was taken to avoid data leakage and ensure compliance with privacy regulations such as GDPR, as all patient data was anonymized.

Beyond model development, the report also outlines a deployment strategy. The proposed system includes a user interface for clinicians, integration with existing infrastructure, and ongoing monitoring of model performance.

Despite promising results, the project acknowledges several limitations. The small dataset size and class imbalance restrict generalizability, and missing data may affect model robustness. Additionally, achieving the target performance threshold of 70% accuracy remains challenging.

The system is designed to provide interpretable predictions, including explanations of key contributing factors, to support informed decision making of clinicians regarding the immunotherapy treatment of their lung cancer patients.
