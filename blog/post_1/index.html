Making of this website

Published: 01 April 2026

Given the way the global economy, geopolitics and the job market are going, you can never be too certain about the smooth sailing of your future, or at least it seems like an increasingly unpredictable time to be alive.

Earlier this month I was trying to polish up my CV just in case, as one does when one is burdened by the current state of the job market and rising prices.

I used to use the Europass format but I remember it being a pain to work with when I first made a CV. I do remember it being cumbersome and limiting, and as it happens, it did not allow me to select "Software Engineering Intern" as a job title, not even anything with the term "software engineering".

I sincerely do not have a very good option about the people who decided to omit that as a job title on the list you could select from. However it's not like I can do anything about it so I chose to take an alternative route. I previously saw some people make their own CV in Word or LaTeX so I decided that it would be a good time follow in their footsteps given the situation.

So, back to my dear friend Overleaf who by now is a vital part of my life as a student. The CV went significant more smoothly from then on as I could basically custom make everything exactly how I wanted it, oh boi and custom make it I did!

After all was well and good with the CV I still had a bit of a feeling that a CV wasn't really saying that much about me, I wanted something that was a bit more encompassing not only of my experience but of me as a person but I didn't really know what to do about that need to express in more in depth. Well, that is until a few days ago :)

I always thought that my professors at university are soooooo cool for having their own website! Most of them forked them from Jekyll & AcademicPages, but nonetheless still cool, some even made their own website, those are on the top tier of being cool in my mind.

Most of them had an impressive amount of quality content on there, and some even had a Blog on there (coolest thing ever in my opinion) with either a collection of the most useful academic information a student would ever need or stories about their personal life from which you'd get an insight into the wisest way of approaching anything.

I am probably forever going to fangirl about the blog posts that my professors have :) but that is beyond the scope of this post :)

I don't know why I didn't think to copy the idea about having a personal website, perhaps I thought it was something reserved only for professors and I'm just a student now, but the reality is that no one can stop you from making your own website, even though you're not an established academic or established in any other way :) It also is a great way to fully express anything about yourself be it profesional or personal or both and fully customizable if you're willing do a bit of coding, but in the age of AI that's very accessible to the not very experienced coders.

So there I was, about 7 days ago, determined to make my own website, I had very basic knowledge about HTML, no clue about CSS or JavaScript and a deep fear of GitHub, which is a bit embarrassing to admit given that I went to a computer science highschool for 4 years and have been doing a tech-oriented degree for the past 2.5 years and I definitely had some courses that required me to deal with the above mentioned :) but worst case scenario I would just do a Google sites and never mention that I tried anything else.

So, I started with a README.md, thankfully Claude was very helpful in my progress out of that stage then I moved on to having another page other than the homepage that was made in HTML, then a couple more pages, then I wanted it to look like it was not made in the 1970s so I incorporated some style in CSS, then some JavaScript to make it all tie together nicer. That felt very light work given that I was basically just designing in Canva and telling Claude to implement what I designed how I wanted it from a functional standpoint, and also aesthetics wise. This honestly did not put me in any difficulty and did not cause much learning on my end.

The real challenge began when I ran out of free messages :) and I am not going to pay for the premium, it was perhaps 3 a.m. and I was way too deeply invested in making this website to let that stop me. ChatGPT has wronged me with coding way too many times for me to trust it, so, I chose the by now vintage way of coding of reading code, googling errors, looking on stack overflow and searching how something works on W3 Schools. This was significantly slower than my previous breezing trough but it did force me to understand what every part of the code does, how it connects, how to identify where an error is, how to fix it, or at least what to try. As you can imagine this led to a lot of learning in the process.

By the time Claude let me talk to it again I had done a big chunk of what I was meaning to implement. I did need a bit more help with a few things, mainly design, but that went pretty straightforward until I got to the 'Things to do in life' page, where again Claude closed its doors and I probably spent a day and a half just on making that page work how I wanted it to work.

What I didn't mention yet is that the more you use up the Claude free messages the less you get next time you get free messages in a conversation. So if at the beginning you can maybe input 20 messages and 10 images/docs or whatever, the next time you get free messages it's more like 10 than 20, and so on until you can just write 2 messages in 12 hours by which time it's no longer useful to use that as its just faster to implement and debugg manually. Also the AI that VS code has built in is good but not really, so I had already grown fed up with it at an early stage.

So eventually, by day 2 and 3 of working on this website my work was about 80% human made, by me :) and by day 4 it was 100%. Which I am quite proud of given how little knowledge I had in the beginning. The whole process took about 6 days during which I learned more than if I had had an entire course dedicated to making a website. I am also proud to say that the 'Things to do in life' page was all me, no AI, in the development, as my idea was apparently too complicated for an AI model to implement how I wanted it to so all the design, formatting and functionality of that page was really handcrafted, strange thing to say 7 years ago but a bit of a flex now. I must admit that the idea for that page was inspired by one of my brilliant professors who had this on his blog, but I think mine at least looks a bit cooler :)

So at the end of this design and development journey, I feel a bit like Bilbo Baggins who was scared to go on the adventure but through it all has learned an invaluable amount.

So yeah, now I am very comfortable with HTML, CSS and JavaScript, and GitHub as well and I will dip my feet in a bit of extra technology to perhaps animate the page a bit better. I will also start working on my bucket list goal of making an aviation project given that now I'm no longer scared of all the coding, debugging and difficult parts of the learning curve it will take.

I think it's also a bit fun to say that I like this website more than that of many of my professors, from a design and functionality perspective, in termen of contents I will not reach their level of wisdom and publications for another 10-20 years at least, but perhaps one day I'll get there.

Also I have to admit having all my resources in one place and being able to show both my academic/profesional side and my personal side feels very fulfilling.

So, as a bit of a conclusion, I am very proud of myself for going on this journey and it indeed feels like a milestone and the opening of the gates of starting something and learning in the process and not learning first and then starting.

I think this is a much better way to go at things, and as someone very wise told me "you should go at life with the confidence of a straight white man", I think I'll start applying that to coding and project development as well :)
